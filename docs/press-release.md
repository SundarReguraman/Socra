# Introducing Socra - your reasoning coach that teaches you how to think through DSA problems, not what to think.

### The Insight isn’t handed to you. It’s discovered — one question at a time.

If you are a student who’s sitting there staring at your laptop screen, blinking and scratching your head — you know the concept, the syntax, all the algorithms, the data structures. But you don’t understand the problem, or you do. You cannot come up with a brute force solution, even if it’s the dumbest brute force approach you can come up with. 

But when it’s time to actually optimize your code logically, you struggle. You try but have no idea where to start. Even the times that you know where to start — I bet you, you don’t know the 2nd step, or the 3rd, or the 4th. But one thing is for sure: you end up stuck somewhere. You try everything, but you realize you are unable to come up with the logic, no matter how hard you have worked or how deep you know your concepts.

Finally, after trying everything that you could possibly think of, you give up. You resort to watching tutorials or pasting the question into an LLM. You understand the logic; the optimized approach is understandable. And you are left to wonder: *Why wasn’t I able to think of that? What is wrong with me? Am I dumb? I feel like I've never actually truly solved a problem on my own.*

---

### This is where Socra comes in. 

Instead of giving you the answer, Socra asks you the exact questions that push you in the right direction. Whenever you are stuck or tend to circle around, you receive a carefully crafted hint — not to reveal the solution, but to guide you toward the right thinking.

Open Socra, paste in the problem that you’re struggling to solve. Socra then reads the problem and asks questions like:
* *“How are the elements in this problem traversed?”*
* *“How many times do you think each element is looked at?”*

These are the exact questions that push students to think about the problem in the right direction. The student's response is constantly evaluated with every question they answer, tracking whether they’re approaching the optimal solution or getting deviated. The student ends up approaching the solution on their own eventually — not only developing their thinking, but also their confidence.
